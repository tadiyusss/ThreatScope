Companies Affected / Famous Cyber Attacked Companies / North America / Yahoo

Yahoo

Overview How it Happened Data Compromised

Overview of the Breach

The Yahoo data breach, which occurred in 2013 and was disclosed in 2016, stands as one of the largest and most significant breaches in history. The breach affected over 3 billion Yahoo accounts, exposing sensitive data such as names, email addresses, telephone numbers, birth dates, hashed passwords, and security questions/answers. The breach was believed to be state-sponsored, with Russia’s FSB (Federal Security Service) suspected to be behind it. Despite being discovered in 2014, Yahoo did not disclose the full extent of the attack until 2016. The breach not only impacted users but also severely damaged Yahoo's brand and reputation, leading to a lower valuation during its acquisition by Verizon.

How the Breach Happened

State-Sponsored Actors and Initial Infiltration

- The attack was attributed to a group of state-sponsored actors believed to be operating under the direction of Russia’s FSB. They exploited a security vulnerability in Yahoo's infrastructure to gain unauthorized access to user accounts. Attackers gained access to Yahoo’s internal network through the use of stolen employee credentials. Once inside, they were able to access the company’s databases and exfiltrate massive amounts of sensitive data. The breach continued for several years before being detected.

Persistent Access and Weak Security Practices

- Yahoo had been using outdated security measures, including weak encryption practices. Attackers were able to bypass these defenses, making the breach worse. Once inside, they used forged cookies to maintain persistent access to users' accounts.

Data Compromised

Personal Account Information

- The data stolen included full names, email addresses, phone numbers, dates of birth, and hashed passwords (though many were encrypted). This information is valuable for identity theft and other malicious activities.

Security Questions and Answers

- Sensitive security information, including answers to security questions (such as "What is your mother’s maiden name?"), was also compromised. This made it easier for attackers to access other accounts or services linked to Yahoo.

Impact on the Company and Customers

- The Yahoo breach had far-reaching consequences, especially for the company’s reputation. As one of the largest data breaches in history, it reduced Yahoo's value during its acquisition by Verizon by approximately $350 million. The breach led to several lawsuits, including class-action suits, and was a focal point in cybersecurity and data privacy discussions. Yahoo's brand was severely damaged, as it was criticized for both the delay in discovering the breach and the lack of timely disclosure. The breach also affected users, many of whom were forced to change their passwords and update security questions. Yahoo also provided credit monitoring services to affected users, but many continued to face risks related to their compromised data. The breach was a key factor in the growing global conversation about the importance of cybersecurity in tech companies, particularly regarding user data protection.
