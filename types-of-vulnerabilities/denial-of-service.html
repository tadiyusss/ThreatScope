Denial of Service (DoS)

Emergance of Hacking Milesstone Incidents Cybercrime in Digital Age

Overview

Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks aim to disrupt the normal functioning of a network resource or service, rendering it unavailable to its legitimate users. This is typically achieved by overwhelming the target system with a flood of malicious requests or traffic, consuming its resources like bandwidth, processing power, and memory. The result is that the targeted server or network becomes slow, unresponsive, or completely crashes, effectively denying service to those who need it. While a single source often characterizes a basic DoS attack, a Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack involves a multitude of compromised systems launching the attack simultaneously, making it significantly more challenging to mitigate.

DDoS attacks leverage a network of compromised computers or devices, often referred to as a botnet, which are controlled by a single attacker. Each compromised device, without the owner's knowledge, sends a stream of malicious requests towards the target. The sheer volume of traffic originating from numerous distinct sources makes it difficult to differentiate legitimate user traffic from the attack traffic.

How it Works

The basic mechanism of a DoS attack involves overwhelming a target system with more requests or traffic than it can handle. This can be achieved through various methods that exploit limitations in the target's resources or the network infrastructure. For example, a SYN flood attack exploits the TCP handshake process by sending a large number of SYN (synchronize) requests without completing the handshake, leaving the server waiting for responses that never arrive and exhausting its connection resources.

Attackers employ DoS and DDoS attacks for a variety of motivations and in diverse real-world scenarios. One common use is to disrupt the services of competitors or organizations with whom they have a conflict, causing financial losses and reputational damage. For instance, e-commerce websites can be targeted during peak shopping periods to prevent customers from making purchases. DDoS attacks are also frequently used as a form of cyber extortion, where attackers demand a ransom to cease the attack and restore normal service. Organizations that rely heavily on online presence and availability are particularly vulnerable to this type of coercion.

Real World Examples

Mirai Botnet Attack
2016

The Mirai botnet, composed of compromised IoT devices like security cameras, launched massive DDoS attacks against DNS provider Dyn, causing widespread internet outages affecting major websites like Twitter, Reddit, and Netflix on October 21, 2016.

GitHub DDoS Attack
2018

GitHub, a popular platform for software development, was hit by a massive DDoS attack peaking at 1.35 terabits per second on February 28, 2018. The attack utilized a technique called "memcached amplification" to amplify the attack traffic.

Cloudflare DDoS Attacks
Ongoing

Cloudflare, a web infrastructure and security company, regularly mitigates large-scale DDoS attacks targeting its customers, demonstrating the persistent nature of these threats against various online services.

Risks and Impacts

Service Disruption and Downtime

The primary impact of DoS and DDoS attacks is the disruption or complete unavailability of critical online services. This can prevent legitimate users from accessing websites, applications, and other essential online resources, leading to significant inconvenience and operational disruptions.

Financial Losses

Downtime caused by these attacks can result in substantial financial losses for organizations, particularly those that rely on online transactions or services for revenue generation. Lost sales, decreased productivity, and the cost of incident response and recovery all contribute to these financial impacts.

Reputational Damage and Loss of Customer Trust

Prolonged or significant service disruptions can severely damage an organization's reputation and erode customer trust. Users may become frustrated and seek alternative services, leading to long-term negative consequences for the affected entity.

Resource Exhaustion and Infrastructure Strain

Defending against and recovering from DoS and DDoS attacks can place a significant strain on an organization's IT infrastructure and security teams. The need to handle massive volumes of malicious traffic and implement mitigation strategies can consume considerable resources and divert attention from other critical tasks.​

Secondary Attacks and Exploitation

DDoS attacks can sometimes be used as a smokescreen for other malicious activities. While security teams are focused on mitigating the denial-of-service, attackers may simultaneously attempt to exploit other vulnerabilities or exfiltrate sensitive data from the compromised systems.
