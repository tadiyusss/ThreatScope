British Airways

Overview How it Happened Data Compromised

Overview of the Breach

In 2018, British Airways experienced a significant cybersecurity breach that exposed the data of over 400,000 customers. The firm was unaware of the problem for two months before a security researcher brought it to their attention. Financial and personal data, including credit card numbers and login credentials, were compromised during the attack. The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) conducted a detailed investigation into the matter. In 2020, the ICO fined British Airways £20 million for inadequately safeguarding customer information. The initial penalties of £183 million was reduced because of the financial strain caused by COVID-19. This incident was one of the most significant sanctions imposed under the EU's GDPR laws

How the Breach Happened

Injection of Malicious JavaScript via Third-Party Scripts

By inserting malicious JavaScript into third-party scripts, hackers took advantage of a weakness in the British Airways website. As the customer provided their information, this script collected it secretly on the payment page. It got past standard security checks since it seemed to be from a reliable source. For weeks, the intrusion went unnoticed, enabling extensive data collecting.

Exploitation of Third-Party Code Vulnerability

The attackers exploited a vulnerability in third-party code used by the site, enabling them to inject a script that mirrored legitimate site behavior. When customers entered their payment details, the malicious script captured and transmitted the information to the attackers. Since the code appeared to be trusted, it was not flagged by security systems, allowing the breach to persist undetected.

Data Compromised

Personal Identifiable Information (PII) Leaked

During the incident, customer names, email addresses, and physical addresses were obtained. Identity fraud and phishing attacks can both leverage this kind of information. Additionally, users are at long-term danger of impersonation when such facts are made public. Consumers were cautioned to remain alert and keep an eye out for any questionable communications.

Financial and Account Credentials Stolen

During purchases, complete credit card information, including CVV numbers, was stolen in real time. Additionally, attackers gained access to login information, including passwords and usernames. This made it possible for customers other than British Airways to have their accounts accessed without authorization. Users who were impacted were required to update their passwords for several services and replace their cards.
