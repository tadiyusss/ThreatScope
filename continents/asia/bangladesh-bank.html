Bangladesh Bank

Overview How it Happened Data Compromised Impact on the Company and Customers

Overview of the Breach

In February 2016, Bangladesh Bank, the central bank of Bangladesh, was the victim of a sophisticated cyber heist that resulted in the theft of $81 million from its account held at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. Unidentified hackers managed to issue 35 fraudulent instructions via the SWIFT network to illegally transfer nearly $1 billion. While most of these transactions were flagged and blocked, five were successful, with $81 million routed to accounts in the Philippines and $20 million to Sri Lanka (which was later recovered). This unprecedented cyberattack highlighted significant vulnerabilities in the bank's cybersecurity infrastructure and the security of the SWIFT messaging system. The incident remains one of the largest bank robberies in history, with most of the stolen funds still unrecovered.

How the Breach Happened

Exploiting Prolonged Network Access

The attackers are believed to have infiltrated Bangladesh Bank's computer network approximately a year before the actual theft. They likely used malware, potentially delivered through phishing emails to bank employees, to gain access and observe the bank's internal processes for payment transfers. This prolonged access allowed them to understand the SWIFT system's operations and obtain the necessary credentials for initiating fraudulent transactions.

Timing, Tactics, and the Flaws in SWIFT Security

On a weekend in February 2016, when Bangladesh Bank's offices were closed, the hackers initiated 35 fraudulent payment requests through the SWIFT network, attempting to transfer close to $1 billion from the bank's account at the New York Fed to various accounts overseas. The timing of the attack during the bank's closure suggests a calculated effort to delay detection. While the majority of these requests were flagged by the banking system, five succeeded due to a combination of factors, including typos in some of the fraudulent instructions that bypassed certain security checks.

Data Compromised

Credentials and Access Protocols

The primary data compromised in this cyber heist were the credentials and access protocols for Bangladesh Bank's SWIFT system. By gaining control over these, the attackers were able to issue fraudulent payment instructions that appeared legitimate to the SWIFT network and the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. This unauthorized access to the bank's financial communication system was the key to executing the theft.

Internal Bank Communications and Operational Details

Additionally, the attackers likely gained access to internal bank communications and operational details during their prolonged presence within Bangladesh Bank's network. This reconnaissance would have provided them with the information needed to craft the fraudulent transfer requests and understand the bank's security procedures, allowing them to bypass some of the initial detection mechanisms.

Impact on the Company and Customers

The Bangladesh Bank cyber heist resulted in a significant financial loss of $81 million, most of which remains unrecovered, severely impacting the bank's financial standing. The incident also caused considerable reputational damage to Bangladesh Bank and raised serious questions about the security of the SWIFT system, a critical infrastructure for global financial transactions. The lack of a clear identification of the perpetrators and the difficulty in recovering the stolen funds led to prolonged investigations and diplomatic efforts. The heist prompted increased scrutiny of cybersecurity measures within the global banking sector and led to calls for enhanced security protocols and information sharing. While the direct impact on individual customers of Bangladesh Bank was not the theft of personal financial data, the breach shook confidence in the security of financial institutions and their ability to safeguard assets held within their systems. The incident also spurred regulatory changes and a greater emphasis on cybersecurity within the financial industry to prevent similar attacks in the future.
