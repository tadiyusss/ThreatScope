Banco De Chile

Overview How it Happened Data Compromised

Overview of the Breach

On May 24, 2018, Banco de Chile fell victim to a sophisticated cyberattack that severely disrupted its operations. Attackers deployed disk-wiping malware, which incapacitated over 9,000 workstations and 500 servers, creating a diversion while simultaneously siphoning $10 million through fraudulent SWIFT transactions. The attack forced the bank to suspend services across nearly 400 branches nationwide for almost two weeks. This cyberattack demonstrated significant vulnerabilities in the bank's cybersecurity infrastructure, and the malware used resembled the Buhtrap malware component known as MBR Killer. The breach highlighted the critical need for robust cybersecurity measures, especially in the financial sector.

How the Breach Happened

  • Malware Development

    The attackers deployed destructive disk-wiping malware that targeted the master boot record (MBR) of Banco de Chile's systems. This wiped the data on thousands of devices, causing major operational disruption. While the bank's IT team worked to recover the affected systems, the attackers executed unauthorized SWIFT wire transfers.

  • Diversion Tactic

    The attackers used the malware as a diversion, focusing bank personnel's efforts on restoring the wiped systems, while secretly conducting fraudulent transactions. This allowed them to bypass typical transactional monitoring, resulting in a loss of $10 million.

  • Malware Origin

    The malware used in the attack was linked to previous attacks on Russian banks, suggesting that the attackers were using familiar tools, potentially linked to state-sponsored or organized cybercriminal groups.

Data Compromised

  • Financial Loss

    The breach led to the loss of $10 million in fraudulent SWIFT transactions that were routed to accounts in Hong Kong. Despite efforts to track and recover the funds, much of the stolen money was untraceable due to international money-laundering practices.

  • Operational Data Loss

    In addition to financial loss, the malware wiped critical data from workstations and servers, halting operations and leaving Banco de Chile's internal systems completely vulnerable for weeks.

  • Malware Origin

    The malware used in the attack was linked to previous attacks on Russian banks, suggesting that the attackers were using familiar tools, potentially linked to state-sponsored or organized cybercriminal groups.

Impact on the Company and Customers

The cyberattack caused substantial operational disruption, forcing the temporary closure of hundreds of branches and leading to a massive rebuild of the bank’s IT infrastructure. Additionally, Banco de Chile experienced significant financial loss due to the fraudulent SWIFT transactions. The attack also exposed critical vulnerabilities in the bank's network segmentation and endpoint protection, leading to heightened concerns within the financial sector about the adequacy of existing cybersecurity measures. Following the breach, the bank faced reputational damage, regulatory scrutiny, and customer distrust. The breach prompted Banco de Chile to commit to significant cybersecurity investments and reforms to prevent similar attacks in the future.
