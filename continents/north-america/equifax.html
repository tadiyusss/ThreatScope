Equifax

Overview How it Happened Data Compromised

Overview of the Breach

In November 2014, Sony Pictures Entertainment suffered a significant cyberattack carried out by a group calling themselves "Guardians of Peace." This attack involved the exfiltration of a massive amount of sensitive data, including employee emails, personal information, unreleased films, and future movie plans. The hackers demanded that Sony halt the release of "The Interview," a satirical film about North Korea's leader, and threatened terrorist attacks on cinemas showing the movie. This brazen act not only compromised a vast amount of data but also had significant repercussions for the company's operations and reputation. Ultimately, the United States government attributed the attack to North Korean state-sponsored actors in retaliation for the film.

How the Breach Happened

Unpatched Apache Struts Vulnerability

Equifax used the Apache Struts framework in its web applications, which contained a critical vulnerability. A patch for the flaw was released in March 2017, but Equifax failed to apply it in a timely manner. Attackers exploited this gap to execute arbitrary commands on the affected server. This oversight allowed them to gain initial access and begin data extraction.

Unpatched Apache Struts Vulnerability

Once inside, attackers maintained persistent access over a period of several months. They bypassed detection by encrypting exfiltrated data and masking their tracks. Equifax's security monitoring systems failed to detect the unauthorized data transfers, enabling one of the largest personal information thefts in history.

Data Compromised

Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

In some cases, attackers accessed driver’s license numbers and credit dispute documents. These contained sensitive financial details submitted to Equifax, significantly widening the breach’s impact.

Driver’s Licenses and Dispute Records

In addition to personal data, the hackers also stole and leaked confidential company information. This included unreleased films and scripts, future film plans, and sensitive email correspondence between employees and film executives. The release of this proprietary information caused significant financial damage and reputational harm to Sony Pictures, disrupting their business operations and strategic planning.

Impact on the Company and Customers

The Equifax breach severely damaged the company’s reputation. Top executives, including the CEO, CIO, and CSO, resigned. The breach led to more than 200 lawsuits, regulatory investigations, and congressional hearings. In 2019, Equifax agreed to a settlement of up to $700 million with the FTC, CFPB, and state attorneys general. The case prompted nationwide discussions on data privacy, consumer protection, and cybersecurity practices. Consumers were offered free credit monitoring, but many continue to face long-term identity fraud risks.
