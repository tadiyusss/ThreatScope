Pegasus Spyware

Initial Discovery Common Early Exploits and Attacks The Development of Security Patching

Overview

A worldwide scandal, the Pegasus spyware show included the illegal monitoring of government officials, journalists, activists, and politicians. It was found in 2016 after a cybersecurity lab received a suspicious communication from a human rights activist in the United Arab Emirates. The attacks happened all across the world, including in the US, Mexico, Saudi Arabia, and France. Without user intervention, Pegasus could infect devices running Symbian, Android, iOS, BlackBerry, and Windows Phone. The Israeli business that created Pegasus, NSO Group, claimed that it only supplied government organizations with the spyware to combat terrorism and criminal activity. However, it was discovered that many democratic and authoritarian governments used it against critics and dissidents. Each installation of the spyware cost hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Method of Attack

Pegasus used "zero-click" exploits to infect devices, which meant that users didn't have to interact with the malware in order to get infected. Attackers installed Pegasus secretly on victims' phones by taking advantage of flaws in apps like WhatsApp. Once inside, the spyware rooted Android devices or jailbroken iPhones to get around security measures. Pegasus provided complete access to encrypted data, cameras, microphones, conversations, and texts. Attackers used social engineering techniques, such as phony communications, to tempt users if zero-click failed.

Impact and Consequences

Impact 1

Journalists and human rights advocates were seriously threatened and some experienced harassment or worse as a result of surveillance. Dissidents' right to privacy was violated when private information about them was gathered without their consent. This weakened international campaigns to defend free expression and human rights.

Impact 2

By abusing Pegasus to spy on political rivals, governments harmed democratic institutions. Because of this, there was mistrust between the people and their governments, which resulted in political instability. The use of spying technology in governance also brought up ethical issues.

Impact 3

In an effort to stop the abuse of Pegasus, tech giants including Apple and Facebook filed a lawsuit against NSO Group. The lawsuits brought widely used platforms' cybersecurity flaws to light. In order to shield consumers from similar risks, they also encouraged businesses to improve technology security.

Impact 4

NSO Group was placed on a blacklist by the US government, which prevented US companies from doing business with them. The extent of Pegasus's threat to national security was demonstrated by this action. It also established a standard for how governments might respond to tech firms that are claimed to have violated human rights.
