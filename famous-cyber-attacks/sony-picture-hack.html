Overview

The Sony Pictures hack occurred in November 2014 and is widely attributed to North Korea state-sponsored hackers. Hackers infiltrated Sony’s network, stole sensitive data, and deployed destructive malware that wiped computer systems. They leaked confidential emails, employee personal data, unreleased films, and internal company documents. The attack coincided with the planned release of The Interview, a film satirizing North Korean leadership. Hackers issued threats against movie theaters showing the film, causing Sony to initially cancel its release. U.S. intelligence agencies linked the attack to North Korea's government. The incident marked a significant escalation of state-sponsored cyber retaliation against corporations.

Method of Attack

The attackers used highly targeted spear-phishing emails to gain access to Sony’s internal network. After establishing a foothold, they escalated privileges and exfiltrated large volumes of data over months. They deployed the Destover malware (also known as WIPALL) to wipe systems and destroy data. The attack combined espionage, sabotage, and public threats, a rare combination in corporate cyberattacks. It highlighted the evolving threat of politically motivated hacking.

Impact and Consequences

Impact 1

Sony Pictures suffered major financial losses due to halted operations and remediation efforts. The cost of recovery was estimated to exceed $100 million. Insurance claims were filed, but the incident still hurt Sony’s bottom line significantly.

Impact 2

Highly sensitive employee information, including Social Security numbers and medical records, was exposed. This led to class-action lawsuits from affected employees. Sony had to offer identity protection services and settle legal claims.

Impact 3

The leaked emails caused reputational damage among Sony executives and the broader entertainment industry. Several internal discussions exposed racial comments and controversial opinions. Public relations teams had to work extensively to repair the company's image.

Impact 4

The hack raised political tensions between the U.S. and North Korea. The FBI formally attributed the attack to North Korea, leading to economic sanctions. It also marked a turning point in U.S. government policy towards cyberattacks from foreign states.
