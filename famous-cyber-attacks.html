WannaCry Ransomware

The WannaCry ransomware exploited a vulnerability in Microsoft Windows' SMBv1 protocol, encrypting files and demanding Bitcoin ransom payments. It spread rapidly like a worm across networks, using the EternalBlue exploit, which was originally developed by the NSA. Victims included hospitals, businesses, and governments in over 150 countries, causing widespread disruption. Microsoft quickly issued patches, but many outdated systems remained vulnerable, amplifying the attack’s impact. WannaCry demonstrated how devastating ransomware could be when combined with worm-like propagation.

Yahoo Data Breaches

Between 2013 and 2014, Yahoo suffered two massive data breaches affecting a total of over 3 billion user accounts. Attackers stole sensitive information such as names, emails, hashed passwords, and security questions, exploiting vulnerabilities through spear-phishing and forged cookies. The breaches were not fully disclosed until 2016–2017, significantly damaging Yahoo’s reputation and valuation. Investigations suggested that nation-state actors were involved in at least one of the breaches. The incident remains one of the largest data breaches in history by number of affected users.

I Love You Virus

The ILOVEYOU virus was a computer worm that spread through email, disguised as a love confession attachment. Once opened, it overwrote files and automatically emailed itself to all contacts in the victim’s address book, causing massive global infection. The worm caused billions of dollars in damages, affecting both personal users and major corporations. Originating from the Philippines, it led to increased awareness and major changes in email security worldwide. It remains one of the most infamous computer viruses in history.

Sony Pictures Hack

In November 2014, Sony Pictures Entertainment was the victim of a cyberattack that led to the theft and leak of massive amounts of internal data. Hackers deployed a wiper malware called Destover, erasing data and rendering systems inoperable. The attack was allegedly carried out by North Korean state-sponsored hackers in retaliation for the planned release of The Interview, a comedy film critical of North Korea’s leader. Leaked emails, scripts, and sensitive employee information caused major financial and reputational damage to Sony. It remains a landmark case in cyber-espionage and cyber-warfare against corporations.

LockBit Ransomware

LockBit is a sophisticated ransomware operation that functions under a Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) model, allowing affiliates to launch attacks using LockBit’s tools in exchange for a profit share. Since emerging in 2020, it has evolved through multiple versions, including LockBit 2.0, 3.0, and LockBit Black, each introducing faster encryption and stronger evasion techniques. The group targets high-value organizations, often stealing data before encrypting it and threatening to leak the data if ransoms aren’t paid. Known for its automated attack tools and tailored ransom notes, LockBit has become one of the most dominant ransomware groups globally. Despite takedowns and warnings from law enforcement, its activity remains high as of 2025.

NotPetya

The NotPetya ransomware encrypts user files when a system reboots because it overwrites the Master Boot Record. For it to carry out its malicious payload, it either plans a reboot or tries to force a system problem. By taking use of the "EternalBlue" vulnerability and using credentials that have been obtained by working with programs like PSEXEC and WMI, the virus spreads internally within networks. Its usage of "EternalBlue" is restricted to internal transmission, in contrast to WannaCry. Since the connected email address for getting the recovery key is no longer functioning, there is no purpose in paying the required ransom. Fortunately, a technique known as a "vaccination" has been discovered which makes the NotPetya ransomware harmless.

Pegasus Spyware

The Israeli cyber-intelligence company NSO Group created the powerful malware Pegasus to stealthily attack iPhones and collect data. It can be secretly installed, frequently with "zero-click" techniques that don't involve the device owner. After installation, Pegasus has access to location information, call records, emails, messages, pictures, and even encrypted files. Pegasus has been used to spy on political personalities, journalists, and activists, despite NSO Group's claim that it only sells it to government organizations for legitimate security reasons. The software received criticism from all around the world for allowing political suppression and human rights abuses. It is one of the most controversial surveillance instruments in the world due to its expensive price and advanced features.

2016 COMELEC Data Breach

A major breach of data at the Philippine Commission on Elections (COMELEC) in March 2016 resulted in the exposure of 55 million registered voters' personal data. Hackers, going by the name "LulzSec Pilipinas," used SQL injection techniques to get access to the database by taking advantage of vulnerabilities in the COMELEC website, such as outdated software and weak passwords. Sensitive information including complete names, addresses, dates of birth, and passport numbers was exposed in the breach and later made public online. The attack caused serious legal and political problems for COMELEC executives and raised concerns about the safety of government systems. A costly investigation, technology changes, and damage to the Philippine electoral system's reputation resulted from the the event.

MySpace XSS

Samy Kamkar's well-known MySpace XSS attack took advantage of a vulnerability in MySpace's security framework. Samy added himself as a friend and hero to any viewer's profile automatically in October 2005 by inserting a little 4KB script into his user profile. In under 20 hours, this script reached 1 million friends after spreading quickly across each compromised user profile. MySpace was forced by the malware attack to temporarily shut down the website in order to eliminate the dangerous code. Even though Samy insisted that his acts were harmless and only for fun, he was later the subject of an investigation, given a $20,000 fine, and put on probation. This incident made clear how crucial it is to properly filter input in order to avoid XSS vulnerabilities.

AWS DDoS Attack

The AWS DDoS attack in Q1 2020, which generated an amazing 2.3 Tbps of traffic, was the biggest attack ever documented at the time. By using a Connection-less Lightweight Directory Access Protocol (CLDAP) reflection technique, this attack far surpassed the previous record. CLDAP servers are an excellent tool for attackers because, if they are exposed or misconfigured, they can respond to spoof requests with significantly larger data packets, thus increasing the assault. Even while this attack received a lot of media attention, many bigger attacks go unreported. The need to implement a zero-trust security model and ongoing DDoS defensive tactics in order to counteract changing threats is highlighted by this incident. Attack volumes have increased quickly, showing the increasing difficulty of DDoS attacks and the urgent need for strong security.
